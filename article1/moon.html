The Mysterious Asymmetry Of The Moon

  Sudha Sravya



Astronomers believe they finally understand how the perpetual Earth-facing near-side and the perpetual far-side of the Moon turned out to be so different over the years after its formation. The image here represents the origin of the moon.This is the original image of the moon, which is captured by NASA.




As we know it is very difficult to know about the symmetry of the moon, whether it is symmetric or asymmetric. Our scientists have done a lot of research to know the solution for this. They used many theories, calculations,etc., and finally concluded that the moon is asymmetric.


It is believed that the Earth and the Moon formed after a Mars-sized body, Theia collided with Earth’s precursor planet referred to as the proto-Earth. Following the collision, Earth turned out to be the larger body, retained enough heat to become tectonically active and eventually evolved into a dynamic planet with an atmosphere and oceans.


On the other hand, Earth’s Moon was smaller. For a long time, scientists believed that the Moon cooled down faster and was in its current state for a long time. But, there has been evidence that indicates the early Moon was much more dynamic consisting of volcanic and magnetic activity occurring until just a billion years ago, which is much later than previously expected.


The first signs of volcanic activity were discovered by NASA's Apollo missions when scientists analysed samples from maria, craters/volcanic features. With these samples, scientists also identified a new type of rock signature called KREEP, an acronym of the elements in it which are potassium, rare‐earth elements (REE, which include cerium, dysprosium, erbium, europium, and other elements which are rare on Earth) and phosphorus.


According to EurekAlert, in a new study published in the journal, Nature Geoscience, an international team of scientists have performed experiments and are modelling to figure out new clues that suggest the asymmetry in geological composition and surface features on the near-side and far-side of the Moon are related to each other because of an important property of KREEP.


Researchers explain that some elements in KREEP, potassium, thorium and uranium are radioactively unstable elements. So, these elements can occur in different forms with variable composition atoms which are known as 'isotopes'. Isotopes being unstable can fall apart in a process known as radioactive decay to form other elements while releasing heat which can melt rocks.


In the study, researchers found that the inclusion of KREEP in rocks lowered their melting points. With models, researchers showed that the expected volcanic activity was compounded because of KREEP and enhanced heating. They have further explained what processes occurred during Moon’s evolution and the timing and volume of volcanic activity at the lunar surface.


Researchers, in their study, explain“Our findings suggest that the nearside geochemical anomaly has influenced the thermal and magmatic evolution of the Moon over its entire post-differentiation history. ”


The study offers an insight into the early stages of evolution of the Earth‐Moon system as co‐author of the study Matthieu Laneuville from Earth‐Life Science Institute at Tokyo Institute of Technology explains in a press release, “Because of the relative lack of erosion processes, the Moon's surface records geological events from the Solar System's early history. In particular, regions on the Moon's near side have concentrations of radioactive elements like U and Th unlike anywhere else on the Moon. Understanding the origin of these local U and Th enrichment can help explain the early stages of the Moon's formation and, as a consequence, conditions on the early Earth.”
